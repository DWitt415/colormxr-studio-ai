
## [EXPOSITION]
Complementary colors aren't just a mathematical relationship — they're built into the physiology of your visual system. The brain actively maintains color equilibrium as you look at things, and when it gets overloaded by a strong color, it generates the complementary color on its own — without any color on the screen at all. These are called **phantom colors**, or the phenomenon of **simultaneous contrast**.

## [EXPOSITION]
This was first documented by the chemist Chevreul in the mid-1800s, and it's the perceptual basis for why complementary colors feel so visually charged together. The effect isn't aesthetic — it's neurological. Your visual cortex is compensating for the imbalanced color input by generating its opposite.

## [EXPOSITION]
You're about to experience this yourself. This exercise works best with the primary and secondary colors, since they're the most saturated. The effect is real — don't be surprised if it takes a moment to kick in.
