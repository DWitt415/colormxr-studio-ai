Colormixing 101

---

OK, let's get started! First I'm going to show you in the colormixer interface how the color sliders work to change color values, and then what the relationships are between the different slider channels.

So first off is the Black slider; this controls all color channels equally, and so the result is a range of neutral Grays from Black to White. Important concept here is that Black, White and Gray are all created by equal proportions of Red, Green and Blue. For this reason, they are considered neutral colors. Note that you are NOT adding or subtracting Black when you use the Black slider, you are changing the color value to Grayscale.

---

Next, we have the primary color channel sliders, the primary color channels are Red, Green, and Blue.
So as we've discussed before, all digital displays, such as computer monitors, TVs, projectors, and phones, use what's known as projective color. 

And underneath the hood, the display color values are created by varying levels of Red, Green and Blue in each pixel in the output. So what we're doing here is we're directly manipulating the primary color components that define the full color output value.

---


Now, I'm going to show you how to mix the primary colors using the Color Mixer interface.
Remember that we are working with additive color here, which simply means adding color to Black. And, Red, Green and Blue mixed at full strength equals White.

First we select a shape. Notice our output shows #000000 meaning pure Black. The Colormxr interface is now controlling the color of this object. 

To make Red is simple enough, just grab the Red slider and slide it all the way to the top. So now as you see it's 255 00. So 255 means maximum Red. It's the reddest Red that you can get and it's the brightest Red because there are no other primary colors mixed into the colors. 

---

Primary colors are defined as having a single full strength color channel and zero color in the other 2 channels.
Primary colors are consideRed primary because they are the fundamental colors that are used to create all other colors in the system.  

---

Next we do the same process with Green. See here now is 0 255 0, no Red, full strength Green and no Blue.

---

And finally, we do Blue and again the same process 00 to 255. 

---

So there you have it, how to mix the primary colors.. Red, pure Red, Green, and Blue.

---


Okay, now from the three primary colors, RGandB we're going to mix the secondary colors, Yellow, Cyan and Magenta

Secondary colors are defined as being full strength mixes of 2 primary colors.
They are the second most fundamental colors, due to their relationship with the Primary color channels

---

Using this shapeset, I ’m going mix the secondary colors.
I’m going to show you now how to use the color clone feature; hold down the shift button to select another shape and change its color to the current color. Now, you are controlling the color of multiple shapes at once, something which will come in handy later!

---


So first up, we're going to create Yellow. So Yellow is 100% Red and 100% Green. Starting with our Red shape, we are going to clone its color onto its neighbor, click on the neighbor to de-select the original, so we don’t change it’s color, and then add Green to make Yellow.

---

Next is Cyan, which is a mix of 100%, Green and 100% Blue. Select the Green shape and then slide the Blue channel alll the way to the top, and there is Cyan.

---

And finally, we have Magenta which is 100% Blue and 100% Red. Select the Blue shape and add Red to it, and we have Magenta

---

Now, you can see, we have a shapeset showing the primary colors, Red, Green and Blue, and the secondary colors, Yellow, Cyan and Magenta.


---



Now that we've mixed the secondary colors using two separate one channel mixes, I'm going to show you one of the key features of the Color Mixer interface and why it has six color sliders.

---

So Yellow, we now know is an equal mix of Red and Green. To make Yellow, you could slide Red and Green individually. 
But with the Color Mixer interface, we can use the Yellow slider instead, which controls the Red and Green color channels equally.

---

So now instead of having to do two separate mixing moves and complicating things, we're able to do it with one slider that controls two channels. And so then we see controlled values of Yellow as it goes from pure Yellow all the way down to Black. In this way, you can preserve secondary color value relationships as you change the color, giving it a more controlled, cohesive and comprehensible appearance.

---

Same thing with Cyan, where we could do it with individual sliders, and maybe arrive at the final color value, or do it intelligently using the Cyan slider to move  through all of the shades of Cyan going all the way to Black.

---

I’ll repeat that process with Magenta, and there we have our 3 secondary colors mixed using the secondary sliders.

---

So to recap, the secondary sliders are actually controlling two primary colors channels at once. This has some interesting implications that you will learn more about in the upcoming lessons.
